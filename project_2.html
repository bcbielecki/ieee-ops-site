Overview

Now that you have created a basic circuit and soldered with a protoboard, let’s explore more advanced topics! You will build two circuits using a 555 Timer IC: (1) a blinking LED circuit, (2) a three-key electronic piano.

Requirements

For readers who are enrolled in the course: In order to receive full marks on your project submission, make sure it meets the following minimum requirements.

555 Blinker

  • The LED in the circuit must blink at roughly a rate of once per second.
  • The LED must be brightly lit when turned ON.
  • The LED must be completely dark when turned OFF.
  • The circuit must be built on a breadboard.

555 Piano

  • The circuit must emit a distinct tone for each of the three buttons pressed.
  • When a button is pressed, the piezo should emit minimal static noise.
  • The circuit must emit tones at a reasonable volume (not too low).
  • The circuit must be soldered to the provided PCB.

Concepts


Ceramic Capacitors

A capacitor is a component that briefly stores electrical energy, which it charges and discharges in bursts. It is not a battery. Batteries store energy at a high density and slowly charge and discharge that energy.

The ability of a capacitor to store energy is determined by its capacitance, measured in farads (F).

The higher the capacitance, the longer it can store energy. Naturally, the capacitor will also take more time to charge and discharge.

Common Uses

Capacitors are often used to smooth the signal output of circuits. This is the case for our 555 Piano. A capacitor in series with the piezo buzzer reduces the noise the from the IC output.

We will also use a capacitor to configure the NE555P IC. The time it takes for the capacitor to charge and discharge determines the delay of the IC.

Capacitor Markings

You can determine the capacitance of a ceramic capacitor from its 3-digit markings. As shown below, the first two digits are multiplied by 10 raised to the power of the third digit. The product is the value of the capacitor measured in picofarads.

Pushbutton

A pushbutton is a type of switch. It completes a circuit when pressed and disconnects the circuit when unpressed.

Pushbutton Legs

The figure to the right shows that the legs labeled 1 are internally connected. The same is true for the legs labeled 2.

Legs 1 and 2 are connected only when the button is pressed.

Symbol

Though pushbuttons have four legs, their schematic symbol reduces the complexity to two terminals (as depicted in the figure to the right).

Piezoelectric Buzzer

A piezoelectric buzzer is a component used to emit sound.

Piezo buzzers come in two varieties: active and passive. Active buzzers have a built-in oscillator to control its frequency. Passive buzzers do not; they have to be fed an oscillating signal.

We will use a passive piezo buzzer, which is non-polar.

Symbol

The symbol on the right depicts a non-polar piezo buzzer. This differs from a speaker, which looks like the traditional volume symbol.

NE555P IC

Printed Circuit Board (PCB)

Breadboards and protoboards are great for prototyping, but when electronics are ready for production, we use printed circuit boards or PCBs. Nearly every device, from digital alarm clock to laptop computer, contains a PCB. It is an assembly, designed with CAD software, that connects individual components.

The benefits of PCBs over prototyping boards are numerous: They’re more compact, more reliable, and enable complex circuit design. Above all, they are easy to mass produce.

For this project, you will solder the 555 Piano to a PCB we specially designed in a software called KiCAD (as seen below). If you are not enrolled in the course, you may alternatively use a protoboard.

Soldering to a PCB is much like soldering to a protoboard, and you may even find it easier. For more soldering tips visit our workshop material here [Webpage Link].
